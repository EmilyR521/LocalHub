Greek verbs change by person (1st, 2nd, 3rd), number (singular, plural), tense, mood, and voice (active, passive). Two main conjugation groups: A (‑ω / ‑ομαι) and B (‑ώ / ‑ιέμαι or ‑ούμαι).

@if (isExpanded('patterns')) {
  • Future uses the particle θα + verb form (e.g. θα λύσω).
  • Present perfect uses auxiliary έχω (have) + infinitive (e.g. έχω λύσει).
  • Past perfect uses είχα (had) + infinitive.
  • Subjunctive often uses particle να (e.g. να λύνω).
  • Conjugation A: verb ending ‑ω (no accent) → active; ‑ομαι → passive.
  • Conjugation B: verb ending ‑ώ (accented) → active; ‑ιέμαι or ‑ούμαι/‑άμαι/‑ώμαι → passive.
}

@if (isExpanded('patterns')) {
  • Präsens and Perfekt cover most everyday German; Perfekt is used for most past events (unlike English, where simple past is common).
  • Präteritum is used more in writing and narrative.
  • Futur I uses werden + infinitive; the present tense is also often used for future.
  • German has four cases: NOM (subject), ACC (direct object), DAT (indirect object, many prepositions), GEN (possession).
